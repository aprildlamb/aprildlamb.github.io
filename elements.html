Population characteristics and Removal Feasibility of Common Carp

Common Carp (Cyprinus carpio) are one of the most widely introduced and ecologically disruptive aquatic species in the world. During foraging, Common Carp uproot submerged aquatic vegetation, alter nutrient dynamics, and re-suspend sediment into the water column, which can drastically reduce water quality and clarity and restructure entire aquatic ecosystems. Removing Common Carp from impaired waterways to improve ecosystem health has shown to reduce or reverse these effects; but, system-wide removals are costly, have historically been limited to smaller lakes and ponds, and vary in long-term effectiveness based on population characteristics such as growth and mortality rate. This leaves a critical gap in our understanding of how to effectively manage Common Carp populations in larger systems. To address this knowledge gap, I evaluated the removal feasibility of Common Carp in Lake Mattamuskeet, a large (16,314-ha) and shallow (~1-m), hypereutrophic lake located in eastern North Carolina.
